Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation, commonly abbreviated as RAG, is a technique introduced by researchers at Meta AI in 2020 to reduce hallucination in large language models. Instead of relying only on knowledge stored in a model's parameters, RAG systems retrieve relevant passages from an external document store at query time and provide them to the model as additional context.

A typical RAG pipeline has two main components: a Retriever, which searches a document collection for relevant passages, and a Generator, which is a large language model such as GPT that produces an answer conditioned on the retrieved passages.

Knowledge Graphs are increasingly combined with RAG systems to form what is often called Graph RAG. In a Graph RAG system, entities and relationships are extracted from documents and stored as a graph structure, allowing the retriever to perform multi-hop reasoning across connected facts rather than relying on similarity search alone.

Multi-Agent systems extend RAG further by dividing responsibilities among specialized agents, such as a retrieval agent, a reasoning agent, and a synthesis agent, which communicate through an orchestration framework such as LangGraph.
